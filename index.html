THE HORSEMEN

Praveen Kumar

“A fragile human outside.
A horse inside.”

Eleven years of learning trust from horses — and from one black stallion who changed everything.

The Black Stallion Who Whispered My Name in Hoofbeats

I have lived eleven years among horses, and still the world feels too small for me everywhere except in their shadow.

Ordinary. Quiet.

A heart that races — never quite falling below 120 — like a drum that forgot how to rest.

Shame has been my oldest companion, a low fog that clings to the skin.

Disgust — first from voices outside me, then echoing inside — has trailed me like dust after a long ride.

I never quite fit in the places people gather.

I only ever felt the air become breathable when a horse stood near, ears flicking, eyes soft, breathing the same slow rhythm as the wind through dry grass.

For the first seven or eight years I trained as though perfection could save me.

Head high, steps even, response immediate.

I was never brutal. Never raised a hand in anger.

But there was always a hidden tension in my fingers — the need to make the horse correct, to prove something, to shape the moment into something flawless.

The horses obeyed. Mostly.

Yet they never melted.

They never turned toward me when the work was done.

They never sought the curve of my shoulder or the open cradle of my palms.

Something was always held back.

Then, three or four years ago, the black stallion walked into my days.

He arrived wearing fear like a second coat.

One wild leap of panic — and he dragged me across the earth.

Not cruelty — only terror.

The dread that he might be harmed fell across my own dread: the pounding heart, the shame that felt eternal, the loneliness of being ordinary and wrong and unseen.

In that breathless instant I could have tightened my grip, shouted, forced obedience.

Instead the old exhaustion rose and whispered: No more.

I was tired of shaping.

Tired of the quiet force that had lived inside every correction.

I wanted to be met with patience, with gentleness, with a presence that never demanded or wounded.

So I offered the black stallion what I had always hungered for: a way that was clean, kind, and without harm.

I let go of perfection.
I let go of force.
I stood still. I breathed. I watched. I waited.

Every day I returned the same: calm, steady, empty of agenda.

My heart still raced, but I would not let him taste it as danger.

My hands stayed soft as summer rain.

My voice stayed low as twilight.

My body stayed open like an unguarded field.

When he tried — even the smallest flicker of courage — I answered with reward.

Carrots melting sweet on his tongue, feed scattered like quiet blessings, and above all, scratching — slow, deliberate, loving strokes along his neck, his withers, his chest.

Those scratches became our vow.

They told him without sound: You are good. You are seen. You are safe.

He began to believe.

When I turned to leave, he learned to summon me back.

One front leg lifted, then the other — higher, higher — a half-piaffe that sang, “It’s time for my treat! Don’t forget me!”

Playful. Insistent. Full of light.

Over time, that small lift unfolded into a full piaffe — proud thunder, rhythmic grace — the instant my shadow touched the doorway.

Now he strikes the stable door with his hoof the moment he senses me near.

If I am late even one day, he calls with loud, urgent nickers until I appear.

And when I do, he rises into that full piaffe — neck curved like a bow, knees high — as though saying: I remember. I’m here. Where were you?

He embraces me now.

Full, warm, whole-body embraces.

He lowers his great head and rests it on my head, on my chest, in my lap, on my shoulder, cradled in the open bowl of my palms.

Sometimes he presses so close I feel his heartbeat slow to find mine, warm breath moving across my skin like the sigh of summer wind over still water.

In those moments, the shame falls away like dead leaves.

The years of feeling ordinary and wrong and unseen simply vanish.

Because this creature — this thousand-pound miracle of muscle and grace — chooses to rest all his trust against me.

That is a truth the universe whispered to someone who never fit anywhere else.

Eleven years.
One black stallion.
A heart that finally learned how to rest.
