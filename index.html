The Black Stallion Who Whispered My Name in Hoofbeats

I have lived eleven years among horses. Still, the world feels too small for me everywhere except in their shadow. Ordinary. Quiet. A heart that races—never quite falling below 120—like a drum that forgot how to rest.

Shame has been my oldest companion, a low fog that clings to the skin. Disgust—first from voices outside, then echoing inside—has trailed me like dust after a long ride. I never quite fit in the places where people gather. I only ever felt the air become breathable when a horse stood near, ears flicking, eyes soft, breathing the same slow rhythm as the wind through dry grass.

For the first seven or eight years, I trained as though perfection could save me. Head high. Steps even. Response immediate. I was never brutal, never raised a hand in anger. But there was always a hidden tension in my fingers—the need to make the horse correct, to prove something, to shape the moment into something flawless.

One wild leap of panic, and he dragged me across the earth. Not cruelty—only terror. The dread that he might be harmed fell across my own dread.

I was tired of shaping, tired of the quiet force that had lived inside every correction. I wanted to be met with patience, with gentleness.

I let go of perfection. I let go of force. I stood still, breathed, watched, and waited.

Then came the day I could not run anymore. I stood next to him.

My body and my mind were still two different people. But something changed.

He is my friend now. He chooses me.
