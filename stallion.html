The Black Stallion Who Whispered My Name in Hoofbeats

I have lived eleven years among horses.

Still, the world feels too small for me everywhere except in their shadow.

Ordinary. Quiet.

A heart that races — never quite falling below 120 — like a drum that forgot how to rest.

Shame has been my oldest companion, a low fog that clings to the skin.

Disgust — first from voices outside, then echoing inside — has trailed me like dust after a long ride.

I never quite fit in the places people gather.

I only ever felt the air become breathable when a horse stood near, ears flicking, eyes soft, breathing the same slow rhythm as the wind through dry grass.

For the first seven or eight years, I trained as though perfection could save me.

Head high. Steps even. Response immediate.

I was never brutal. Never raised a hand in anger.

But there was always a hidden tension in my fingers — the need to make the horse correct, to prove something, to shape the moment into something flawless.

The horses obeyed. Mostly.

Yet they never melted. They never turned toward me when the work was done. They never sought the curve of my shoulder or the open cradle of my palms.

Something was always held back.

Then — three or four years ago — the black stallion walked into my days.

He arrived wearing no mask. No fear. Nothing but quiet excitement, vigilance, and curiosity — like a second coat of rawness and unfiltered truth.

He wasn’t stubborn, but he made me question how I wanted to be treated. And everything began to change.

One wild leap of panic — and he dragged me across the earth.

Not cruelty. Only terror.

The dread that he might be harmed fell across my own dread: the pounding heart, the shame that felt eternal, the loneliness of being ordinary and unseen.

In that breathless instant, I could have tightened my grip, shouted, forced obedience.

Instead, the old exhaustion rose and whispered: No more.

I was tired of shaping. Tired of the quiet force inside every correction.

I wanted to be met with patience, gentleness, with a presence that never demanded or wounded.

I didn’t even realize I was already handling correctly. My mind was chaos — but my body stayed present.

Until one moment — he rose, standing on his hind legs toward me.

And I misunderstood him.

A gesture that was only asking for release — I saw it as something else.

So I made a decision.

I gave him what I had always wanted: a way that was clean, kind, and without harm.

I let go of perfection.

I let go of force.

I stood still. I breathed. I watched. I waited.

Every day, I returned the same: calm, steady, empty of agenda.

My heart still raced — but I would not let him feel it as danger.

My hands stayed soft like summer rain. My voice stayed low like twilight. My body stayed open like an unguarded field.

And when he tried — even the smallest flicker — I rewarded him.

Carrots melting sweet on his tongue. Feed scattered like quiet blessings. And above all — scratching.

Slow. Deliberate. Loving.

Those scratches became our vow.

They told him, without words: You are good. You are seen. You are safe.

He began to believe.

But then came the days that broke whatever was left of me.

One day — he dragged me again. Harder.

The rope burned into my skin. The ground slammed my body.

For two minutes, I was certain: This is the end.

I would die.

I laughed later. But the terror stayed in my bones like a stain.

Years apart — another day — he reared high… walked on his hind legs… and jumped over me.

I have never been so terrified in my life.

Not because I thought he would kill me.

Because I thought something might happen to him.

I could not lose that horse.

I would rather die than lose him.

After that… I couldn’t even stand beside him.

I went into my room.

Twenty days.

Cursing myself in ways no human should speak. Feeling worthless. Dependent. Finished.

This was the end. My last time ever standing beside a horse.

The pain in my chest — I can still feel it writing this.

Anger so thick it felt like I could choke on it.

I believed every ugly word I said to myself.

But still… every few days — seven or eight times in those twenty days — I went back to see him.

Even when I could barely breathe.

And every time… he showed me something.

Head up. The exact movement I had rewarded before.

Like he was saying: Look. I remember. I’m still here.

I didn’t understand then.

I thought I was broken.

I didn’t see — he was waiting.

Then came the day I couldn’t run anymore.

I stood next to him.

Heart pounding like fists inside my ribs.

Thousands of thoughts. Anger. Fear. Grief.

But I wasn’t afraid of dying.

I was afraid of losing him.

My mind and body were two different people.

Mind: chaos. Body: calm. working. present.

And then I understood something:

Listening is not hearing.

Hearing and doing nothing is absence.

Real listening is hearing — and acting.

With intelligence. With effort. With presence.

So I gave him what I needed:

Generosity. Space. Patience.

Very little force.

And he responded. Beautifully. Voluntarily.

But something was still missing…

Until I stopped projecting my storm onto him.

I became silent.

Non-threatening — even when I was scared. Consistent — even when I wanted to break.

Every tension in him, I felt. Every mistake, I let pass.

I waited. I breathed. I controlled myself.

And something changed.

My resistance became observation.

My body and mind finally walked together.

And he changed.

He lifted his legs higher — to impress me. He raised his head the way I loved. He called for me when I was gone.

He chose me.

He trusted me enough to let me scratch him. Even playfully bite when I got it wrong.

My only mistake?

I misunderstood his rare rears.

They were never threats.

They were requests. For release. For play.

I saw them wrong for so long.

He didn’t change me in one moment.

He waited. Month after month.

While I broke open.

He held the mirror — until I could finally look.

Now I see. Not just horses. People too.

Their intentions. Their silence. Their games.

I stay calm on the outside — even when everything inside is loud.

I observe. I act. I listen.

He is my friend now.

He calls for me. He moves for me. He chooses me.

And I am here.

Writing this. Preparing for the next horse.

Because the journey only began when I stopped running from myself.

If you have read this far — thank you.

It is not easy to write. It is not easy to live.

But if something in you felt this…

Stay.

There is more.
