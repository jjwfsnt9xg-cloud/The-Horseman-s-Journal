The Black Stallion Who Spoke My Name Through Hoofbeats

I have spent eleven years of my life among horses.
And still, the world feels unbearably small anywhere outside their presence.

Everywhere else, I am ordinary.

Quiet.

A body that exists, but never quite belongs.

My heart has never learned how to rest.
It beats endlessly — fast, relentless — like a drum that has forgotten silence, never dipping below its quiet panic.

Shame has walked beside me longer than any human ever has.
It clings like a low, invisible fog, settling into the skin, refusing to lift.

Disgust came later.
First, it lived in the voices of others.
Then it found a home inside my own mind, echoing louder, sharper, more convincing.

I never fit where people gather.
Never understood the rhythm of their closeness.

But with horses…
Something changed.

The air softened.
It became breathable.

Standing beside them — watching their ears flick, their eyes soften, their breath slow and deep like wind through dry grass — I felt, for the first time, that I could exist without resistance.

For the first seven or eight years, I trained like someone chasing salvation.

Perfection became my language.
Head high.
Steps precise.
Responses immediate.

I was never cruel.
Never raised my hand in anger.

But there was always something hidden in me —
a tension in my fingers,
a quiet insistence,
a need to shape everything into something flawless.

The horses listened.
They obeyed.

But they never gave.

They never melted into me.
Never turned toward me when the work ended.
Never sought the warmth of my shoulder or the quiet safety of my hands.

Something in them always stayed guarded.
Held back.

And then, three or four years ago…
he arrived.

The black stallion stepped into my life without warning — and without disguise.

There was no mask on him.
No fear hidden beneath obedience.

Only rawness.
A quiet electricity of curiosity, vigilance, and something almost childlike — as if he wore truth itself as a second skin.

He wasn’t stubborn.
He was honest.

And somehow, in his presence, I began to understand how I had always wanted to be treated.

Everything began to shift.

Then came the first moment that shattered me.

A sudden surge — panic, explosive and uncontrollable —
and he dragged me across the ground.

It wasn’t cruelty.
It was fear.

But in that moment, his fear collided with mine —
my racing heart,
my buried shame,
the lifelong feeling of being wrong, unseen, and alone.

I could have reacted the old way.
Tightened my grip.
Forced control.
Demanded obedience.

But something in me broke open instead.

No more.

I was tired.

Tired of shaping everything.
Tired of forcing softness out of something that needed space.
Tired of the invisible pressure inside every “correction.”

So I gave him what I had always longed for myself —
gentleness without condition,
patience without demand,
presence without harm.

Even when my mind spiraled — dark, chaotic, filled with thoughts I could not control — my body stayed.
It listened.
It remained.

And when he reared toward me, when I misunderstood him, when I failed to see that he was only asking for release…

I still chose softness.

I let go of perfection.
I let go of force.

I stood still.
I breathed.
I waited.

Day after day, I returned the same.

Calm.
Steady.
Empty of expectation.

My heart still raced, but I refused to let him feel it as danger.

My hands stayed soft — like summer rain.
My voice stayed low — like the last light of evening.
My body stayed open — like a field with no fences.

And when he tried — even the smallest attempt — I answered.

Carrots dissolved sweetly on his tongue.
Feed scattered like quiet offerings.
And my hands…

Always my hands.

Scratching slowly, deliberately, with care — along his neck, his chest, his withers.

Those scratches became our language.

You are good.
You are seen.
You are safe.

And slowly… he began to believe it.

But healing is never a straight path.

There came days that broke me all over again.

One day, he dragged me harder than before.
The rope burned into my skin.
The ground hit me again and again.

For two full minutes, I thought I would die.

I laughed about it later.
But fear like that never really leaves — it settles into the bones, quiet but permanent.

Years later, another moment —

He rose high, towering, walking on his hind legs…
and then leaped clean over me.

I have never felt terror like that.

Not because I feared death.

But because I feared losing him.

That fear was deeper than anything else.

After that day, I couldn’t even stand beside him.

I disappeared.

For twenty days, I stayed in my room —
breaking myself down with words no human should ever hear,
let alone say to themselves.

Worthless.
Dependent.
Finished.

I believed my life with horses was over.

That I would never stand beside one again.

The pain in my chest became something physical —
tight, suffocating, constant.

And yet…

Every few days, I forced myself to go back.

Seven… eight times in those twenty days.

And every single time, he showed me something I didn’t understand then.

He lifted his head.
Moved exactly the way I had once rewarded.

Look. I remember.
I’m still here.

But I couldn’t see it.

I thought I was the broken one.
I didn’t realize…

He was waiting for me.

Then came the moment I couldn’t run anymore.

I stood beside him again.

My heart pounded violently — like fists from the inside trying to escape my ribs.

My mind was chaos.
Anger.
Fear.
Grief that refused to loosen its grip.

But beneath all of that…

I wasn’t afraid of him.

I was afraid of losing him.

My body and mind were no longer the same place.

My mind screamed.
My body listened.

It stayed present.
It gave the aid.
It waited.
It rewarded.

And in that moment, something finally became clear.

Listening is not hearing.

Hearing and doing nothing is emptiness.

True listening is action —
intentional, aware, responsive.

So I chose differently.

I gave him space.
Generosity.
Softness with boundaries.

And he responded — beautifully, willingly.

But the real change didn’t happen until I stopped placing my storm onto him.

I became quiet.

Even when I was terrified, I remained non-threatening.
Even when I wanted to break, I stayed consistent.

I felt everything in him — every shift, every tension.

And I stopped punishing mistakes.

I waited.
I breathed.

Not now.

That silence… became something powerful.

Resistance turned into observation.
Chaos turned into awareness.

My body and mind began to move together — not perfectly, but honestly.

And then…

He began to change.

He lifted himself higher — not from pressure, but to impress.
He moved the way I loved — not because he had to, but because he chose to.

He called for me.
Beat the door when I was gone.
Made sounds when I stayed away too long.

He chose me.

Over everyone else.

He trusted me enough to be playful — even correcting me gently when I misunderstood him.

And my greatest mistake?

Thinking his rearing was defiance.

It wasn’t.

It was communication.
A request.
Sometimes even an invitation to play.

I misunderstood him for so long.

But he never left.

He didn’t change me in one moment.

He stayed.
Month after month.

Holding a mirror in front of me…
until I could finally look.

Through him, I learned to see —

Not just horses,
but people.

Their intentions.
Their patterns.
Their hidden motives.

I learned to stay calm while everything inside me shakes.
To observe without reacting.
To truly listen — and respond.

Now, he is not just a horse to me.

He is my friend.

He calls for me.
Moves for me.
Waits for me.

He chooses me.

And I am still here —
writing this,
standing at the edge of the next beginning.

Because the real journey didn’t start with him.

It started the moment I stopped running from myself.

If you’ve stayed this long…

Thank you.
This story isn’t easy to carry.
It isn’t easy to live.

But if something inside you recognized even a fragment of it…

Stay.

There is always more waiting to be understood.
