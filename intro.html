The Black Stallion Who Whispered My Name in Hoofbeats

I have lived eleven years among horses. Still, the world feels too small for me everywhere except in their shadow. Ordinary. Quiet. A heart that races—never quite falling below 120—like a drum that forgot how to rest.

Shame has been my oldest companion, a low fog that clings to the skin. Disgust—first from voices outside, then echoing inside—has trailed me like dust after a long ride. I never quite fit in the places where people gather. I only ever felt the air become breathable when a horse stood near, ears flicking, eyes soft, breathing the same slow rhythm as the wind through dry grass.

For the first seven or eight years, I trained as though perfection could save me. Head high. Steps even. Response immediate. I was never brutal, never raised a hand in anger. But there was always a hidden tension in my fingers—the need to make the horse correct, to prove something, to shape the moment into something flawless. The horses obeyed, mostly. Yet they never melted. They never turned toward me when the work was done. They never sought the curve of my shoulder or the open cradle of my palms. Something was always held back.

Then, three or four years ago, the black stallion walked into my days. He arrived wearing no mask, no fear—nothing but quiet excitement, vigilance, and curiosity to explore, like a second coat of rawness and unfiltered truth. He wasn’t stubborn, but he made me realize how I wanted to be treated, and everything began to change.

One wild leap of panic, and he dragged me across the earth. Not cruelty—only terror. The dread that he might be harmed fell across my own dread: the pounding heart, the shame that felt eternal, the loneliness of being ordinary and wrong and unseen. In that breathless instant, I could have tightened my grip, shouted, or forced obedience. Instead, the old exhaustion rose and whispered: no more.

I was tired of shaping, tired of the quiet force that had lived inside every correction. I wanted to be met with patience, with gentleness, with a presence that never demanded or wounded. I was still trying to find a way to handle him, unaware that I was already handling him correctly. My mind filled with the worst thoughts, but I still chose to offer him what I had always hungered for: a way that was clean, kind, and without harm.

I let go of perfection. I let go of force. I stood still, breathed, watched, and waited. Every day, I returned the same—calm, steady, empty of agenda. My heart still raced, but I would not let him feel it as danger. My hands stayed soft as summer rain, my voice low as twilight, my body open like an unguarded field.

When he tried—even the smallest flicker of courage—I answered with reward: carrots melting sweet on his tongue, feed scattered like quiet blessings, and above all, scratching—slow, deliberate strokes along his neck, his withers, his chest. Those scratches became our vow. They told him without sound: you are good, you are seen, you are safe. He began to believe.

But then came the days that broke whatever was left of me. There was a day when he reared high, walking on his hind legs, and then jumped clean over me. I have never been so terrified in my life. Not because I thought he would kill me, but because I now realize how, in fear, I mistook his gesture of asking for release as danger. Not once—he did it twice—and I could not bear it. The feeling of not being able to handle a horse, instead of understanding him, still hurts me every day.

At the same time, I was terrified that something might happen to him. I could not lose that horse. I would rather die than lose him. That fear made me vulnerable. I remember screaming that I could not handle the horse anymore, and the fear was real—I had never felt so scared of anything in my entire life. I laughed later, but the terror stayed in my bones like a stain.

After that incident, I could not even stand beside him. I went into my room. Twenty days. Cursing myself in ways no human should speak to another. Feeling worthless, dependent, finished. This felt like the end of my career—the last time I would ever stand next to any horse. The pain in my chest—I can still feel it now, even while writing this. Anger so thick I could choke on it. I spoke to myself with the ugliest words, and I believed them.

But every few days—seven or eight times in those twenty days—I forced myself to go see him, even when I could barely breathe. And every single time, he showed me what I had taught him. Head up. The exact movement I had rewarded over and over again. He did it like he was saying: look, I remember, I’m still here. I didn’t understand then that he was trying to reach me. I thought I was the broken one. I didn’t see that he was waiting.

Then came the day I could not run anymore. I stood next to him. My heart pounded so hard it felt like fists hitting my ribs from the inside, trying to break out and flood everything. Thousands of thoughts—anger, disappointment, fear, grief that wouldn’t leave. But I was not afraid he would kill me. I was afraid of losing him.

My body and my mind were still two different people. My mind was full of chaos—cursing, replaying every failure, every pain. My body was silent, alert, doing the work, giving the aid, waiting, rewarding. And in that moment, I understood something for the first time: true listening is not just hearing. Hearing and doing nothing is not listening—it is absence. Real listening is hearing and then acting on what is heard, with intelligence, effort, and presence.

I decided to give him what I had always wanted someone to give me: generosity, very little force, and space to choose. He did everything beautifully, voluntarily. But the deep satisfaction was missing until I stopped projecting my storm onto him. I stayed silent, non-threatening even when I was terrified inside, consistent even when I wanted to scream. Every tiny tension in his body, I felt it. Every wrong step, I did not punish. I waited, I breathed, and I told my own mind: not now.

That silence became something powerful. My resistance slowly turned into observation. My body and mind began to walk side by side—confident, yet alert. It may have taken me twenty days to stand beside him again, but this time I was what the horse had shaped me into—strictly alert, constantly aware, my mind and body working together with him. I could sense more, react less, and truly see.

Soon, validation came—the horse corrected himself. I simply stayed, watched, and gave clean, quiet instructions. Not calmness, but clarity.

If a horse jumps or rushes toward you, it is not trying to harm you. It may be testing you, asking for release, or simply trying to play. Do not misjudge your horse the way I once did.

He started to change in front of me. Lifting his leg higher to impress me. Raising his head the way I loved. Beating the door until I came. Making sounds when I was gone for two or three days. He chose me over his owner. He trusted me so deeply that he let me scratch him—even biting me gently when I did it wrong, not in anger, just in play.

My only mistake was reading his rare rears as a threat. They were not threats. They were him asking for relief, or asking to play. I misunderstood him for so long.

The black stallion did not change me in one big moment. He waited—month after month—while I broke open. He held the mirror until I could finally look without running away. Through him, I learned to see—not just horses, but people too. Their actions, their intentions, their agendas, their manipulations, their games.

I learned to stay calm on the surface while everything inside me pounds. To observe without reacting. To listen—and then act—instead of just hearing and remaining silent within.

He is my friend now. He beats the door until I come to scratch him. He piaffes when he sees me, asking for attention. He makes sounds if I have been away. He chooses me.

And I am here, writing this, preparing for the next horse. Because the journey only truly began when I stopped running from myself.

If you have read this far, thank you. It is not easy to write. It is not easy to live. But if any part of it touches something in you, stay. There is more.
