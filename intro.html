The Black Stallion Who Spoke My Name Through Hoofbeats

I have spent eleven years of my life among horses.
And still, the world feels unbearably small anywhere outside their presence.

Everywhere else, I am ordinary.
Quiet.
A body that exists, but never quite belongs.

My heart has never learned how to rest.
It beats endlessly — fast, relentless — like a drum that has forgotten silence, never dipping below its quiet panic.

Shame has walked beside me longer than any human ever has.
It clings like a low, invisible fog, settling into the skin, refusing to lift.

Disgust came later.
First, it lived in the voices of others.
Then it found a home inside my own mind, echoing louder, sharper, more convincing.

I never fit where people gather.
Never understood the rhythm of their closeness.

But with horses…
Something changed.

The air softened.
It became breathable.

Standing beside them — watching their ears flick, their eyes soften, their breath slow and deep like wind through dry grass — I felt, for the first time, that I could exist without resistance.

For the first seven or eight years, I trained like someone chasing salvation.

Perfection became my language.
Head high.
Steps precise.
Responses immediate.

I was never cruel.
Never raised my hand in anger.

But there was always something hidden in me —
a tension in my fingers,
a quiet insistence,
a need to shape everything into something flawless.

The horses listened.
They obeyed.

But they never gave.

They never melted into me.
Never turned toward me when the work ended.
Never sought the warmth of my shoulder or the quiet safety of my hands.

Something in them always stayed guarded.
Held back.

And then, three or four years ago…
he arrived.

The black stallion stepped into my life without warning — and without disguise.

There was no mask on him.
No fear hidden beneath obedience.

Only rawness.
A quiet electricity of curiosity, vigilance, and something almost childlike — as if he wore truth itself as a second skin.

He wasn’t stubborn.
He was honest.

And somehow, in his presence, I began to understand how I had always wanted to be treated.

Everything began to shift.

Then came the first moment that shattered me.

A sudden surge — panic, explosive and uncontrollable —
and he dragged me across the ground.

It wasn’t cruelty.
It was fear.

But in that moment, his fear collided with mine —
my racing heart,
my buried shame,
the lifelong feeling of being wrong, unseen, and alone.

I could have reacted the old way.
Tightened my grip.
Forced control.
Demanded obedience.

But something in me broke open instead.

No more.

I was tired.

Tired of shaping everything.
Tired of forcing softness out of something that needed space.
Tired of the invisible pressure inside every “correction.”

So I gave him what I had always longed for myself —
gentleness without condition,
patience without demand,
presence without harm.

Even when my mind spiraled — dark, chaotic, filled with thoughts I could not control — my body stayed.
It listened.
It remained.

And when he reared toward me, when I misunderstood him, when I failed to see that he was only asking for release…

I still chose softness.

I let go of perfection.
I let go of force.

I stood still.
I breathed.
I waited.

Day after day, I returned the same.

Calm.
Steady.
Empty of expectation.

My heart still raced, but I refused to let him feel it as danger.

My hands stayed soft — like summer rain.
My voice stayed low — like the last light of evening.
My body stayed open — like a field with no fences.

And when he tried — even the smallest attempt — I answered.

Carrots dissolved sweetly on his tongue.
Feed scattered like quiet offerings.
And my hands…

Always my hands.

Scratching slowly, deliberately, with care — along his neck, his chest, his withers.

Those scratches became our language.

You are good.
You are seen.
You are safe.

And slowly… he began to believe it.
